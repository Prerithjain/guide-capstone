How Does Our Groundwater Project Work?

A complete beginner's explanation — no technical background needed. We break every data type, every process, and every output into plain everyday language.

🏙 Bangalore 🌄 Ramanagara 🌾 Mandya 📚 Capstone Batch 6

🎯 What Is This Project Trying to Do — In One Sentence?

Imagine underground water is stored in rocks beneath your feet. This project wants to predict which areas of Bangalore, Ramanagara, and Mandya will run out of underground water in the next 10–25 years — and warn people before it is too late.

🔮 We are building an AI-powered early warning system for underground water.
🏦

Think of underground water like a bank account

Rain is the income (money coming in). Borewells, farming, and city taps are the withdrawals (money going out). In Bangalore right now, people are withdrawing 9.4 times more than what comes in. The account is emptying. This project builds a system that tells you: "At this rate, your account will hit zero by 2038." — and shows you where to dig a recharge well to refill it.

1

The Data We Need — and Why

Think of each data type as a clue we're collecting to solve a mystery: "Where is the water going?"

📏
Historical Groundwater Levels (2014–2024)
10 years of measurements from 1,767 wells across Karnataka
Must Have
🔷 What is it?
Scientists have been measuring how deep the water is underground at thousands of wells across Karnataka — twice a year, every year since 2014. Think of it like measuring how full a well is every six months.
🟢 Why do we need it?
This is the answer sheet our AI learns from. It sees 10 years of "the water was at this depth in June, and then this depth in November" — and learns the pattern. Without this, the AI has nothing to learn from.
🟣 How do we get it?
We download it for free from the CGWB government website (Central Ground Water Board). They publish a report every 6 months with all well depths for all districts.
🌧️
Daily Rainfall Data
Grid-based daily rain measurements covering the entire region
Must Have
🔷 What is it?
India's weather department (IMD) has divided the country into small 28km × 28km boxes. They measure how much rain fell in each box every single day. We use this for our three districts.
🟢 Why do we need it?
Here is the key fact: In our rocky region, 70–80% of all underground water refilling happens in just 2–5 heavy rain storms per year. If we only use monthly rainfall totals, we miss these critical moments. We need daily data to catch those big storms.
🟣 How do we get it?
Downloaded from IMD Pune's official website — the same place the weather forecast comes from. Over 100 years of daily data is available for free.
🏞️
KRS Reservoir Water Level
Daily water storage data from Krishna Raja Sagar Dam, Mandya
Extreme for Mandya
🔷 What is it?
KRS Dam is the big dam on the Cauvery River in Mandya. Every day, the government records exactly how much water is stored in it — like checking how full your water tank is.
🟢 Why do we need it?
The canals from KRS Dam carry water through Mandya's farms. As the water flows through the canals and into the soil, some of it seeps underground and refills the aquifer. When KRS is low, this seepage stops — and underground water in Mandya directly falls. The dam level is a direct remote control for Mandya's groundwater.
🟣 How do we get it?
Downloaded daily from the Central Water Commission (CWC) — India's official river and dam monitoring authority.
🌡️
Temperature & Evapotranspiration (ET)
How hot is it? And how much water is evaporating?
High Priority
🔷 What is it?
Temperature is straightforward — how hot the weather is. Evapotranspiration (ET) is a fancy word for: how much water is being sucked up from the soil and plants into the air due to heat. Like how a puddle dries up faster on a hot day.
🟢 Why do we need it?
When it gets hotter, more rain evaporates before it can soak underground. Studies show every 1°C rise in temperature causes an extra 8 cm of underground water to disappear per year. This is why climate change is making the situation worse even when rainfall amount doesn't change.
🟣 How do we get it?
Temperature from IMD weather stations. ET is calculated using a standard formula (FAO Penman-Monteith) that combines temperature, humidity, wind, and sunlight into one "how much water evaporated today" number.
🪨
Specific Yield — How Much Water Can the Rock Hold?
The single most important property of Mandya's underground rock
Extreme Criticality
🧽 Sponge vs Stone Analogy: A sponge holds a lot of water and releases it slowly when you squeeze. Mandya's underground rock is like stone — it barely holds any water at all (only 0.05% — that means from 1 litre of rock, only 0.5 mL is water!). So when farmers pump just a little bit from it, the water table drops dramatically — like squeezing a nearly dry stone.
🔷 What is it?
Specific Yield (Sy) measures what percentage of the underground rock is actually filled with usable water. Mandya's granite rock has Sy ≤ 0.05% — one of the lowest values in India.
🟢 Why do we need it?
It tells us HOW FAST the water table will drop when people pump. With such a tiny Sy, even pumping a small borewell in Mandya can cause a 10-metre drop in water table. This is why Mandya's "Day Zero" is so close — the rock can barely store water.
🟣 How do we get it?
From the CGWB NAQUIM report (a government aquifer mapping study) and a scientific model called AMBHAS-1D that was used specifically to map the Upper Cauvery Basin where Mandya lies.
💨
Transmissivity & Hydraulic Conductivity — How Fast Does Water Flow Underground?
Measures the "speed limit" of water movement inside the rock
High Priority
🔷 What is it?
Hydraulic Conductivity (K) = how easily water flows through rock (like how fast water drains through sand vs clay). Transmissivity (T) = how much water can flow through the full thickness of rock per day. In Bangalore South, it varies from 2 to 280 m²/day — a 140× difference!
🟢 Why do we need it?
Our computer simulation (MODFLOW) uses this to calculate exactly which direction water flows underground and how fast. Without this, the simulation would give wrong answers — like predicting water flows north when it actually flows south.
🟣 How do we get it?
From CGWB borewell pump test records — when engineers drill a test borewell, they pump it and measure how fast the water level drops. This drop speed tells us T and K for that location.
🛰️
Lineament Density — Finding Cracks in the Rock from Space
Satellite-detected fracture lines that act as underground water highways
High Priority
🔷 What is it?
The hard granite rock below our region has millions of tiny cracks called fractures or lineaments. These cracks are like tiny tunnels where underground water travels and collects. Satellite images can detect these crack patterns from space because they show up as faint lines in the landscape.
🟢 Why do we need it?
In our region, there is almost NO water in the solid rock — all the water is in the cracks. So areas with more cracks = more water. This is the most important factor for finding good borewell locations and for mapping where water can flow underground.
🟣 How do we get it?
We download Landsat satellite images from USGS Earth Explorer (free) and use GIS software to trace the visible crack patterns. We then calculate how many cracks per square kilometre — that is the "lineament density" value.
🏙️
Land Use / Land Cover (LULC) — What is the Ground Made Of?
Are we looking at concrete, farms, forests, or water bodies?
Must Have — Bangalore
☔ Rain on Different Surfaces: When rain falls on a forest or park, it soaks into the ground. When it falls on concrete roads and buildings, it becomes runoff — flows into drains and is lost. Bangalore's green cover went from 28% in 2010 to just 2.9% in 2023. That means nearly all rain now runs off without recharging underground water.
🔷 What is it?
A map showing what type of surface covers each area — buildings, roads, farmland, forest, lakes, or open land. Updated every year using satellite images.
🟢 Why do we need it?
It tells us where rain can soak into the ground (recharge zones) and where it cannot (concrete areas). Also needed to predict sugarcane farming areas in Mandya — sugarcane uses 1,500–2,000 mm of water per season, the highest of any crop.
🟣 How do we get it?
We use Sentinel-2 satellite images (free, updated every 5 days). Software colours each pixel based on what it sees — green = vegetation, grey = urban, blue = water. This process is called "image classification."
⛰️
Digital Elevation Model (DEM) — A 3D Map of the Land
Used to figure out where water naturally flows and collects
Medium Priority
🔷 What is it?
A DEM is a digital 3D height map of the land surface — every 30m × 30m square on the map has an elevation value (how many metres above sea level). From this, we calculate: Slope (how steep?), TWI (how likely is water to collect here?), and Drainage Density (how many streams drain this area?).
🟢 Why do we need it?
Water always flows downhill. The DEM tells us where rain water will naturally collect and soak in versus where it will flow away. Low flat areas with high TWI values are natural recharge zones — ideal spots for building artificial recharge structures.
🟣 How do we get it?
Downloaded for free from NASA's SRTM mission — it mapped the entire Earth's elevation from a Space Shuttle. Resolution: one measurement every 30 metres.
🚰
Groundwater Extraction Rates — How Much Are We Pumping?
The amount of water being taken out by borewells every day
Must Have
🔷 What is it?
Total amount of underground water pumped out by all borewells across each taluk — for city supply, farming, industries, and households. Bangalore went from 600 MLD (million litres per day) in 2020 to 1,457 MLD in 2024. That is 2.4 times increase in just 4 years.
🟢 Why do we need it?
This is the "withdrawal" from our bank account. Without knowing how much is being taken out, we cannot calculate when the account hits zero. It is the primary driver of "Day Zero" predictions.
🟣 How do we get it?
From the Karnataka Groundwater Directorate's Antharjala portal — they publish taluk-level annual extraction vs recharge estimates. Also from the OpenCity research dashboard for Bengaluru.
🌍
Climate Change Scenarios (RCP 4.5 & RCP 8.5) — What Happens in the Future?
Two possible futures: moderate warming vs extreme warming
Must Have — Phase 4
📅 Two Future Stories: Scientists around the world have created two "what if" stories for the future. RCP 4.5 = "Moderate" — humans reduce carbon emissions a bit. RCP 8.5 = "Worst Case" — humans keep burning fossil fuels at the current rate. We run our model in both futures to see how bad groundwater stress gets in each.
🔷 What is it?
Projections from global climate models (CMIP6) that predict future rainfall, temperature, and evaporation for South India from 2025 to 2050. "RCP" stands for how much heat-trapping gas humans are emitting — 4.5 is moderate, 8.5 is severe.
🟢 Why do we need it?
To answer the question: "Even if extraction stays the same, will there be less rain and more evaporation in 2040?" Yes — and under RCP 8.5, high potential groundwater zones could shrink by 15–22% by 2040. This is Phase 4 of the project.
🟣 How do we get it?
Downloaded from CORDEX South Asia — a project where climate scientists have taken global models and zoomed into South Asia to get localised future climate data at 25km resolution.

The 5-Step Process — From Raw Data to Final Prediction

Here is how all that data gets turned into a map showing which areas will run out of water

1

🗂️ Step 1: Data Collection & Cleaning

First, we go and gather all the data from the sources explained above — government websites, satellite portals, dam authorities. This is like gathering all the ingredients before you start cooking.
🍳 Cooking analogy: Before you cook, you gather onions, tomatoes, spices. Here, we gather rainfall CSVs, borewell depth Excel files, satellite images, and reservoir level PDFs from various government portals.
📥
Download CGWB pre- and post-monsoon bulletins for all 3 districts (2014–2024)
🌧️
Download IMD daily gridded rainfall files for the region
🛰️
Download Landsat / Sentinel satellite images from USGS Earth Explorer
🧹
Clean the data: Remove duplicate entries, fill in missing months using averages, convert all units to the same format (e.g., all depths in metres)
📊
Organise everything into a structured table: one row per month per well, with all variables as columns
2

🤖 Step 2: Teaching the AI (LSTM Model Training)

Now we teach a special type of AI — called LSTM — to recognise patterns in the data. It learns: "When rainfall is high in August AND the previous month's water level was at 12m, then the water level in September is usually 10m."
🧠 Learning analogy: LSTM is like a student who studies 10 years of exam results and notices patterns — "Whenever it rained heavily in June AND the dam was full, groundwater recovered by November." It then uses these patterns to predict the future. The "Long Short-Term Memory" part means it can remember what happened 3–6 months ago, not just last month.
📚
Training: Feed the AI data from 2014–2021 (7 years). It sees thousands of examples of "these inputs led to this groundwater level"
✅
Validation: Test it on 2022–2023 data it has NEVER seen. If it predicts correctly, it has learned the right patterns
🎯
Testing: Final check on 2024 data. We measure accuracy using R² (how well predictions match reality — 0.95+ means 95% accurate)
🔮
Output: The trained AI can now predict groundwater levels 12 months into the future for any well in our 3 districts
3

🏗️ Step 3: Underground Water Flow Simulation (MODFLOW)

The AI predictions tell us water levels at specific wells. But we need to know what is happening between the wells — how is water flowing underground? For this, we use a physics-based computer simulation called MODFLOW.
🗺️ GPS map analogy: The LSTM gives us the water level at specific points — like a GPS giving you coordinates. MODFLOW fills in the map between those points — like how Google Maps draws all the roads and terrain between two GPS points. It simulates the entire underground as a 3D grid and calculates water movement in every single 1km × 1km cell.
📐
Build the model: Divide the 9,200 km² study area into a grid. Each 1km × 1km cell has two layers — shallow weathered zone (0–30m) and deeper fractured zone (30–250m)
⚙️
Set parameters: Assign each cell its rock properties — Specific Yield, Hydraulic Conductivity, Transmissivity — from the geological data we collected
💧
Set boundaries: Tell the model where water enters (rain recharge, canal seepage from KRS) and where it exits (pumping borewells, river flow)
🎛️
Calibrate: Run the simulation and compare its output to the actual measured well levels. Adjust parameters until the simulation matches reality (target: R² ≈ 0.95)
4

🗺️ Step 4: Making the Vulnerability Map

Now we combine all the GIS layers (satellite data, geology, slopes, cracks) with our AI predictions to create a colour-coded map showing which areas are most at risk of running out of groundwater.
🚦 Traffic light analogy: Just like a map shows traffic as green (free), yellow (slow), red (jam) — our vulnerability map shows groundwater zones as green (safe), yellow (warning), red (critical danger zone). Areas that appear red need urgent intervention — like building a recharge pond or reducing borewell usage.
📡
Prepare 14 GIS layers: Geology, lineament density, LULC, slope, TWI, drainage density, NDVI, extraction rates, soil type, rainfall, depth to water table, annual decline rate
🌲
Run LightGBM / Random Forest: These are AI algorithms that combine all 14 layers to score every location — "How vulnerable is this 1km cell to running dry?" They achieve >90% accuracy
🗾
Kriging interpolation: This is a mathematical technique that smoothly fills in the gaps between our prediction points to create a continuous map surface (like how weather maps show smooth rainfall patterns, not just dots)
🏷️
Output: A map of all 3 districts divided into Very High / High / Moderate / Low risk zones for groundwater depletion
5

🌍 Step 5: Future Scenario Prediction ("Day Zero" Mapping)

Finally, we run the whole model into the future (2025–2050) using the climate change projections. This tells us WHEN and WHERE groundwater will hit critical levels — the so-called "Day Zero" — when the water in the shallow aquifer is essentially gone.
📉 Stock market analogy: Imagine a stock that is falling. A financial analyst runs two scenarios: "If the economy improves moderately (RCP 4.5), the stock stabilises by 2035. But if the recession worsens (RCP 8.5), the stock crashes by 2038." We do the same for groundwater — running both moderate and worst-case climate futures to show when each taluk hits "Day Zero."
🌡️
Feed future climate into the model: Replace historical rainfall and temperature with the CMIP6/CORDEX future projections for 2025–2050
📈
Project extraction growth: Assume Bangalore's population keeps growing at 3.5%/year and extraction increases proportionally
📅
Identify "Day Zero" taluk-by-taluk: Which taluks hit critically low groundwater levels first? (e.g., Nagamangala in Mandya and Bangalore South are likely earliest)
✅
Test solutions: Run "What if we build 500 recharge ponds in Mandya?" and "What if Bangalore reduces extraction by 20%?" — and show how much each intervention delays Day Zero

📖 Glossary of Terms

Every technical word used in this project, explained in plain language

Aquifer
The underground layer of rock that holds water — like a natural underground tank. Our project focuses on the "shallow aquifer" which is the top 0–30 metres of weathered rock.
Groundwater Level (GWL)
How deep you have to dig or drill before you hit water. If GWL is 15m, you need to go 15 metres underground to find water. Higher GWL = closer to surface = good. Deeper = bad.
LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory)
A type of AI that is good at learning from sequences of data over time. Like how you can predict tomorrow's weather better if you know the last 7 days, not just today. LSTM "remembers" 3–6 months of past patterns.
MODFLOW
A free software made by the US Geological Survey that simulates how water flows underground in 3D — like a flight simulator but for groundwater. Used worldwide by hydrogeologists.
Specific Yield (Sy)
The percentage of rock volume that is actually available water. Mandya's rock has Sy ≤ 0.05% — only 0.5 mL of water per litre of rock. This is why it depletes so fast.
Transmissivity (T)
How easily water flows horizontally through rock — measured in m²/day. High T = water flows fast (like a river in rock). Low T = water barely moves. Bangalore South varies from 2 to 280 m²/day.
Recharge
The natural process where rainwater soaks into the ground and refills the underground water supply. Like topping up a tank. In our region, 70–80% of recharge happens during just 2–5 big rain storms each year.
Evapotranspiration (ET)
Water that evaporates from soil and transpires from plant leaves into the air — "lost" water that never reaches the underground aquifer. Increases with temperature and is a key reason climate change reduces groundwater recharge.
Lineament Density
The number of rock fractures (cracks) visible per square kilometre from satellite images. In hard-rock areas like ours, these fractures are the main pathways where groundwater flows and collects.
LULC (Land Use / Land Cover)
A map showing what the ground is covered with — buildings, roads, farms, forests, or water. Crucial for knowing where rain can soak in (forests, parks) vs where it runs off (concrete, roads).
TWI (Topographic Wetness Index)
A number calculated from the landscape shape — how much water is likely to naturally accumulate in this spot. High TWI = natural water collecting zone = good recharge area. Calculated from DEM data.
DEM (Digital Elevation Model)
A 3D digital map of the land surface height. Every 30m × 30m square has an elevation value. Used to calculate slope, drainage paths, and TWI. Downloaded free from NASA's SRTM mission data.
CGWB
Central Ground Water Board — India's government agency that monitors groundwater levels at 1,767 wells across Karnataka. Publishes free reports every 6 months. Our primary data source.
RCP 4.5 / RCP 8.5
Two future climate scenarios. RCP 4.5 = moderate warming (humans reduce emissions). RCP 8.5 = severe warming (business as usual). We run our model in both futures to see the range of possible outcomes by 2050.
CMIP6 / CORDEX
International projects where climate scientists pool their models to create standardised future climate projections. CORDEX zooms into South Asia specifically. We use their data for Phase 4 climate scenario analysis.
LightGBM / Random Forest
Types of AI algorithms particularly good at combining many different variables (our 14 GIS layers) to make predictions. Like having 500 different analysts each look at the data, then voting on the final answer — that is what Random Forest does.
Kriging / EBK
A mathematical technique for filling in a map between known data points. If we know groundwater levels at 50 wells, Kriging estimates what the level would be at every location between them — creating a smooth continuous map.
Day Zero
The day when an area's groundwater hits critically low levels — meaning borewells stop yielding water. Like Cape Town in 2018 almost running out of reservoir water. Our project predicts which taluks will hit Day Zero first and when.
MAR (Managed Aquifer Recharge)
Deliberately refilling underground water by building structures that help rain soak in faster — check dams, percolation ponds, borewell recharge pits. Our DSS recommends the best locations for MAR in the 3 districts.
DSS (Decision Support System)
Our final web dashboard that presents all predictions in a user-friendly interface. Different views for farmers, water managers, and policymakers — so each group gets the information relevant to their decisions.
